## 34 Years of SoyBase: From Genetic Maps to Pan-genome Resources for Soybean Improvement

In the early 1990s, USDA ARS anticipated a coming genomic revolution and recognized that crop research needed a centralized, community accessible data resource. In 1992, Dr. Randy Shoemaker was selected to lead the effort to develop a soybean genetic and genomic database. At that time, SoyBase existed entirely on a single computer in the Shoemaker lab. SoyBase was developed to serve as a central repository for soybean RFLP, RAPD, SSR, and SNP marker datasets.

Before the World Wide Web (WWW) was established, soybean researchers and breeders used the [ACeDB]( https://github.com/richarddurbin/acedb/) browser to view genetic maps. The ACeDB program required the data be stored on the local computers. To view the soybean genetic maps collected by the SoyBase curator data was distributed directly to researchers on CDs.  To remain current, researchers requested updated CDs, which the SoyBase curator mailed via USPS.


### First Steps Toward Connectivity

By 1994, soybean researchers were citing SoyBase as the place where datasets were deposited or available via SoyBase’s FTP server—a major step forward for digital data sharing. Early scientific publications began referencing SoyBase as a source [Zhu et al.,(1994)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00282754), [Lorenzen et al., (1995)](https://acsess.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2135/cropsci1995.0011183X003500050012x)).

At that time, SoyBase was technically “online,” but accessible only to users who knew the specific IP address. An article in [Agricultural Research Magazine (1999)](https://agresearchmag.ars.usda.gov/1999/feb/map/) described this early online presence and SoyBase’s pioneering role in USDA’s efforts to provide genetic map resources over the internet.

### Preparing for the Web Era 

By 2003, SoyBase needed to evolve to a platform that could both deliver data over the World Wide Web (WWW) and support remote, real time access. SoyBase became a scalable, maintainable, and query capable architecture. With new pages, search interfaces, and data exploration tools. These changes transformed SoyBase into a dynamic, interactive web resource accessible to researchers around the world.


### Incorporating Genomic Data: 

SoyBase expanded dramatically in 2008 with the release of the first complete soybean genome assembly and gene model annotation from the cultivar Williams 82. This assembly—named Glyma1—represented a transformative moment for soybean research and for SoyBase. With genome scale data available, SoyBase introduced powerful new online tools, including: GBrowse for exploring the genome and viewing gene models and markers and BLAST for sequence similarity searches. This shift marked SoyBase’s evolution from a genetic map database into a fully integrated genomic science resource.










SoyBase directly supports USDA ARS missions by enabling breeders, researchers, and stakeholders to explore soybean genetic diversity, accelerate trait discovery, and enhance breeding efforts for resilience, nutrition, and sustainability. It ensures data accessibility and fosters innovation across the agricultural research community.
